Entry 001

It was too late to save Eamon Ellis.

The bastard had run away whilst his father had been plotting his escape plan.

And now, less than an hour before processing, he let himself be found, dragged back to his father’s feet and the escape plan rendered useless.

Eamon's hands and legs were tied up with multiple zip ties, and he was carried in by four of his father’s men like some kind of pillowcase you’d tuck under your arm.

Then he felt himself be dumped, more or less gently, to the ground.

They were in one of his penthouse suites, surrounded by typical luxury and the blessing of the golden horizon as his father would refer to it.

He would argue it was more burning and blinding to the eyes than a beautiful morning glow.

“Found…you..” His father laughed. He stood, hands behind his back, towering over his body like he’d won.

“A little late”, Eamon smirked, his blonde hair tousled over his face, and partially covering his black coloured eyes.

“You think this is funny don’t you?” His father used the tip of his boot to part his hair up the centre and out of his face.

“You laughed first.” He answered back. He couldn’t actually see his father, just the black tip of his boot and the grey pant of his leg. That was, until his father finally booted his ribs hard enough to tip him onto his back. “Ow-”

“I told you not to run.” His father said flatly, now coming into view. He was dressed simply today, not his usual grandeur, or accessories, just a simple grey suit, and his black-grey hair combed back. The overall lack in grandeur indicated stress.

Eamon went silent.

“Oh? Where’s that smirk now?”

Nothing.

“Why’d you run?”

Nothing still.

“Pick him up.” He ordered the guards. They roughed him to his feet, he stood there. Black eyes staring each other down.

“I asked you. A question.” His father spoke.

“What?” He finally responded. Eamon was a little rude, he’d admit that, but this time he’d actually forgotten what his father had been asking in the first place. He hadn’t really been listening.

“Why’d you run?” He sighed, more fed up from the stress this thing gave him than angry.

“Because.” Eamon said, “I’m due for service, and if you hide me, you’ll get us all killed.”

“Killed?” He laughed, “Killed? I? Resthanov Ellis, Killed?”

“Yes, you, Resthanov Ellis.” He paused. “You and everyone else who holds that same second name.” Sometimes he wondered if his father should be checked for delusions.

Resthanov shook his head. “You don’t even know what you’re doing, you don’t know what they’re going to do to you once you get there. Your life is ruined.” As soon as he’d gotten the notice of Eamon’s selection, he’d made preparations to send him away, eventually have a new identity made; if plastic surgery was required, he’d do that too. He’d been trying to protect the idiot. But like mentioned earlier, he had run, long enough for it to be too late to hide him, or even have a fake death registered. “Whatever, sit him down.” He muttered orders to his men.

Eamon found two hands pushing him down into a seat which just happened to appear under him. Another seat was dragged to his father, who sat opposite. Seconds later, with a wave of his hand, the restraints around Eamon came off, he rubbed his raw wrists and stood up to stretch. The guards almost flinched, not into fear, but a brace in case he tried to run again.

He sat back down.

“Father?” He said. His father wasn’t saying anything, just looking at him with big resigned eyes as if he might not see him again.

“Oh come on, you’re being dramatic, sure it's mandatory, and for life, but I’ll clock out of work like any normal person. And then I come home and have dinner with you guys.” Plus, rich kids get better paying, leadership roles after training. It was like forced success, in its own way.

“How is that any better than working RIGHT HERE?!” His father barked. “UNDER ME, WHERE YOU BELONG?! WITH FAMILY?!”

Working for the government wasn’t better, but he wouldn’t say that or his father might make a more hysterical last minute plan than the previous one. Eamon wasn’t a great fan of mixing family with business either way. The last project he’d worked on with his cousin had actually lost the company a couple of million. And that was because the goal post had shifted into stabbing each other in the back, metaphorically speaking.

“Father, I want to go.” He lied. “It’s mandatory, I’ll still get to come home when my shift ends, I’ll have great pay.” And he’d stop his idiot dad from getting their entire family killed. Win win.

And then, as if some weird afterthought, “Where’s mum?” He said. He’d want to say goodbye.

“Your mother is on her way.”

As if on cue she walked through the doors, face all angry and scrunched up, in black heels and a flowing red dress. The sound of the heels clacking against the floor coupled with her expression was practically foreboding death. He was definitely sure she was going to smack him with the purse, and he wasn’t wrong.

“Idiot!” She smacked him across the head with her red handbag, “Running? Where on earth did you get that idea from?”

It wasn’t a particularly new or original one, teenagers have been doing it for centuries. “Ow-” He winced, “I’m sorry!” He was not.

But then she hugged him tight, burying his head in her neck like he was some kind of infant that needed to be joined with its mother or its whole world would fall apart. But he didn’t dare pull away.

“We just wanted to help, you didn’t even let us do that.” His mum said.

He shrugged. “I wanted to go.”

“You could’ve just said that, you didn’t have to run.” She said, but as the words left her lips the three of them knew that if Eamon had let those words come out his mouth he would’ve been locked away until they could deal with it.

“I know.” An idea struck Resthanov. “Just claim illness.”

“Do I look ill?” Eamon scoffed. He was fit, cardio and weightlifting, five days a week. And a personal trainer that would correct even the slightest tilt in posture.

“He means mental illness.” His mum said. “Not a bad idea.” She nodded at her husband approvingly.

No one had assumed he would cause chaos and buy just enough time so that when he was found-

A knock on the door. And then a definitive slide open. A man in a simple white shirt with a black suit walked in holding a tablet. Several men trailed in behind him like they were infesting the room. “Eamon Ellis? It’s time to be processed.”

Resthanov’s heart lurched.
